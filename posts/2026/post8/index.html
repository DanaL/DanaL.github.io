Idle thoughts about writing games

  1. Basic
  2. But I had made a teeny, tiny world!
  3. Sort of graphics
  4. Ultima IV
  5. Exploration
March 8, 2026

My first computer was a Commodore Vic-20, followed a few years later by a Commodore 64 and if you were a nerdy kid fascinated by the machines, you perused the spiral-bound manuals that game with them and discovered the Basic programming language, which effectively was the C64's shell. I remember the C64 manual had chapters on sprite graphics, which I messed around with and did some simple animations, but I never fully grokked what was going on with them.

To be honest, I was more interested in playing games than making them. Partly because it's way easier to play games than to make me, but also it seemed like Basic was for baby, toy stuff and real programs were written in 6502 assembly, and assembly was Mysterious and Difficult and For Adults. That said I learned much later on that a bunch of commercial adventure games were in fact written in Basic! I just wasn't precocious enough I guess.

By the way, 50 Years of Text Games is a very cool read if you are into this genre, and the history of computer games in general.

Basic

Nevertheless, I mucked around with a Basic a bunch. Like lots of nascent programmers, I did find it really cool that I could give the computer commands and it would do the things I asked it do. I remember making little programs that would ask your name and say hello, a painstaking ASCII animation of a stickman throwing a ball through a basketball hoop. (The C64's ASCII variant was pretty robust) I would type in programs from magazines and modify them a bit.

I played a lot of text adventures back then and attempted to make my own little games. My first efforts were just question/answer things. Like:

You are in a cave. There is an orc here.

Attack the orc? (y/n)

Come to think of it, the popular Choose Your Own Adventure books and the Steve Jackson Fighting Fantasy books were probably also an influence/inspiration.

However, those 'games' where extremely simplistic and never felt like 'real' games to me.

I'm not sure if I ever played any Infocom games on my C64, but I remember playing one about a pirate and another about a vampire, which I think must have been the ones made my Adventure International.

Those were much cooler than my question/answer games because you typed in instructions to the game and the (sometimes snarky) narrator responded as though you were having a conversation with it.

You are in a cave. There is an angry orc sharpening his axe.

> attack orc

How do you attack the orc?

> throw rock at rock

You throw a rock at the rock. He yelps and runs away deeper into the cavern.

At some point I figured out how to parse strings – this is such a trivial bit of code but at the time it felt like a major level up to me, I was so stoked – and make a very simple two word parser. I could handle commands like:

> west

You walk to the west. There is a lantern here.

> get lantern

But more complicated parsing like the Infocom games did was beyond me.

But I had made a teeny, tiny world!

So once I got basic parsing working, I made a teeny little game. Well, I wouldn't call it a game since there was nothing to do in it. A tech demo? But I scribbled down a little map of my 'world' on graph paper (my first game design??). It would have been a 4x4 grid or 5x5 grid of locations. I wrote descriptions for each location and wrote up a little text adventure-esque thing where you could walk around and look at stuff.

But let me tell you, actually being able to WALK AROUND this little world I had created felt amazing. My own teeny little pocket universe.

After that, I wrote a few small text adventures where you had a goal and things to do, items and an inventory. They weren't very good but were fun to make.

Sort of graphics

One neat feature of the C64 was that you could replace the default in-memory font with your own. I can't remember exactly how it worked, but I remember discovering this and then setting out to make a 'graphical' RPG. Something along the lines of Phantasie, I think the first crpg I ever played, and possibly my first exposure to rpgs at all. A family friend briefly introduced me to D&D around the same time, but I can't actually remember which happened first.

So, I created my own font and little 8x8 pixel art glyphs for trees, mountains, monsters, the player, etc. IIRC, I implemented basic character creature, a small wilderness area you could explore and the first dungeon. This wasn't a roguelike, the wilderness and dungeon were all hand-written in the Basic code.

Once again, it was absolutely thrilling having my own little, kind-of graphical world to walk around it with a monster or two to fight and some gold to pick up.

I wish I still had the source code for that one! In my dim memories, I kind of simulated having functions via GOTO statements. I was careful to have a 'main' program and you'd GOTO the combat code for fights, etc. Commodore Basic had only global variables so you had to be careful about everything and you wanted to avoid dreaded spaghetti code where you GOTO everywhere and your program becomes a mess to debug. There wasn't even a real code editor. You'd just type lines of Basic on the C64's command line and they'd be added to whatever Basic program you had in memory. It was a pretty painstaking way to code and when I discovered Pascal on the Mac our high school had it was a real revelation.

Ultima IV

A hugely influential game was Ultima IV. I'll level with you: my copy was a pirated copy. It was just part of the C64 culture that you had boxes and boxes of copied 5 1/4" floppy disks. If your friend had a game you didn't, OF COURSE they'd make a copy for you. What 13 year old had money for games?? Apparently on release in 1985 Ultima IV cost $59.95 USD :O Pretty wild how the cost of games has barely changed numerically in 40 years. I'll need to remember this when I feel sticker shock at $90 (Canadian) prices...

I'm sorry Lord British, I have bought Ultima IV more than once since then and Ultima VI was one of the first games I ever saved up for and purchased...

But one side effect of playing a pirated copy was that I did not have the map that game with a legal copy of the game. I had to wander around the game world exploring, eventually getting the lay of the land and just discovering routes between the towns. Eventually you could get a ship and sail around and get a better sense of how the world was shaped.

Importantly, the act of simply exploring the world and slowly learning about it was as fascinating to me at the plot of the game. I'd do entire sessions just walking around, fighting random monsters and seeing the sights. What's on the other side of those mountains? If I sail my boat up the river will I find some new secret?

Another 80s game I absolutely loved back then was Seven Cities of Gold. It had procedurally generated worlds and I had the same fascination with sailing around and uncovering its world. "What's over here? Does this river go anywhere cool?" In Seven Cities of Gold you were playing a Spanish conquistador and these days it should be regarded as rather problematic. Apparently the main designer intended the game to encourage friendly contact with the indigenous villages you found but...I don't remember that reflected at all in the gameplay...

Exploration

The colour scheme I've picked for Delve's overworld is, I'm pretty sure, an echo of Ultima IV's wilderness. Or at least trying to recreate the feeling and texture of it. What I'd would really love to recreate is that feeling I had of a world you could explore, that sense that there might be secrets just up the road, around the bend.

I don't think that game is Delve since I think I've settled on its scope. But for a different game I wonder how far you could get with procedural generation without running into the 10,000 bowls of oatmeal problem. I'm imaging a game with a larger wilderness, several villages you could travel between and little dungeons and ruined towers and such. Games like Qud and Dwarf Fortress show you can generate histories, but I haven't played enough to know if they overcome the oatmeal.

But can you generate a dungeon that has things like So-and-so's Blade of Blading in it and then seed rumours among the nearby villagers? Generate micro-plots like goblins or bandits attacking the nearby village and the player can help out?

I think if I made a game like this, I would enjoy it, but in part because I know I'll be thrilled by having created a little world that my little guy can walk around in an explore. I wonder if it could be sufficiently to other people?

Can a world be generated that is sufficiently fresh each you play and can elements be remixed in interesting ways to hold up to many, many replays?

That's for the future, once Delve is post 1.0 and I don't feel guilty for not focusing my main efforts on that. I feel like I'd need to spend a lot more time on design and planning and prototyping various systems.

Mainly, I wanted to put into words how motivating and fun just making your own little world is, even if it is tiny (like 16 locations in a not-quite-a-game text adventure). And also just to notice that aspect of computer games – exploring a fresh new world – that really appeals to me.
